The Making of Indian Constitution

1934 – MN Roy proposed the idea of a Constituent Assembly.

1935 – INC announced the setting up of a Constituent Assembly.

1940 – Demand for a Constituent Assembly accepted in the August Offer.

1942 – Cripps Mission proposal rejected due to the Muslim League’s demand for two separate states.

1946 – Constituent Assembly was constituted as per the Cabinet Mission Plan.

Formation of Constituent Assembly

Year – November 1946.

Objective – Transfer of sovereign power from the British government to Indian leadership.

Representation – Provincial states and Princely states.

Composition – 296 British India + 93 for Princely states (Total = 389).

Mode of Election – Partly elected (indirect election by Provincial Assembly members) + Partly nominated by Princely states.

Result of Election – INC (208 seats), Muslim League (73 seats), Independent (15 seats).

Important Facts Related to Constituent Assembly

1st Meeting – 9th December 1946.

Interim President – Dr. Sachchidananda Sinha (due to his age).

1st Meeting Attendance – 211 members. Princely states did not attend.

Elected President – Dr. Rajendra Prasad (24 January 1950).

Vice Presidents – H.C. Mukherjee and V.T. Krishnamachari.

Duration – 2 years, 11 months, and 18 days.

Adopted Constitution – 26th November 1949.

Cost – ₹64 lakhs spent on drafting.

Logo – Elephant.

Secretary – H.V.R. Iyengar.

Legal Advisor – B.N. Rau.

3rd Meeting – Constituted by Jawaharlal Nehru (13 December 1946).

National Flag – Adopted on 22 July 1947, designed by Pingali Venkayya.

Original Constitution – 395 Articles, 8 Schedules, 22 Parts.

Current Constitution – 448 Articles, 12 Schedules, 25 Parts.

    1. Firoz Meharuddin v. Sub-Divisional Officer (1960)

      2. The article defines who is a citizen at the time the Constitution came into effect. The Court decided that if someone migrated to Pakistan after March 1, 1947, they could not claim Indian citizenship, even if they met other criteria. This case highlighted that citizenship rights are not absolute and can be affected by migration status, emphasizing the importance of intention to reside permanently in India to qualify for citizenship

    2. Kulathil Mammu v. State of Kerala (1966)

      3. The petitioner argued that he was a citizen because he met the criteria of being born in India or having parents who were born there. However, the Court clarified that even if someone meets the conditions of Article 5, they could still be denied citizenship if they migrated to Pakistan after March 1, 1947, as outlined in Article 7. This case emphasized that citizenship is not absolute and can be affected by migration status.

    3. State of Bihar v. Kumar Amar Singh (1955)

      4. In the State of Bihar v. Kumar Amar Singh (1955) case, a woman known as Kumar Rani traveled to Pakistan in July 1948 for what she claimed was medical treatment. She argued that since her husband remained in India, she should still be considered an Indian citizen under Article 5 of the Constitution, which grants citizenship based on domicile and birth in India. However, the Supreme Court found her claims unconvincing, noting that she had actually migrated to Pakistan after March 1, 1947. Article 7 states that anyone who migrated to Pakistan after this date cannot claim Indian citizenship. The Court concluded that even if her husband's domicile was India, it did not grant her citizenship because Article 7 overrides Article 5 in such cases. Thus, her migration disqualified her from being recognized as an Indian citizen.

    1. Sarbananda Sonowal v. Union of India (2005)


    The Sarbananda Sonowal v. Union of India (2005) case is significant in the context of citizenship and immigration laws in India, particularly concerning Assam. Here’s a simplified explanation

    background

    The case arose from concerns about illegal immigration in Assam, especially from Bangladesh. The Assam Accord was signed in 1985 to address these issues, which included provisions for granting citizenship to certain migrants.

    key Issues

    Section 6A of the Citizenship Act: This section allows individuals who entered Assam before January 1, 1966, to be considered citizens. Those who entered between January 1, 1966, and March 25, 1971, can also gain citizenship but cannot vote for ten years. Petitioners argued that this provision was discriminatory and violated Article 6 of the Constitution, which sets July 19, 1948, as the cut-off date for citizenship.

    Supreme Court's Role

    The Supreme Court had to decide whether Section 6A was constitutional and if it unfairly impacted the rights of Assamese citizens by allowing illegal immigrants to become citizens. The court framed several questions regarding the implications of this provision on Assamese culture and political rights

    outcome

    The case has been significant in shaping discussions around citizenship laws in India, particularly in Assam, and is part of ongoing debates about immigration and national identity

    focus

    This case challenged the constitutionality of the Illegal Migrants (Determination by Tribunals) Act, 1983 (IMDT Act)

    Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib Sehravardi (1981)


    The Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib Sehravardi (1981) case is significant in understanding the relationship between private educational institutions and the government in India.

    background

    Ajay Hasia was the principal of a private school, and Khalid Mujib Sehravardi was a student who was denied admission.The case arose when the student challenged the school's decision, claiming it violated his rights.


    Key Issue

    The main question was whether private schools, even though they are not government-run, could be considered "State" under Article 12 of the Indian Constitution.This determination would mean that private schools must also respect fundamental rights, like the right to education.


    supreme court decision

    The Supreme Court ruled that private educational institutions receiving substantial government aid could be classified as "other authorities" under Article 12.This means they are accountable for upholding fundamental rights and can be challenged in court if they violate these rights.


    Implication

    The ruling established that private schools must operate fairly and cannot discriminate against students.It reinforced the idea that even non-governmental entities performing public functions are subject to judicial review and must respect citizens' rights.

    In simple terms, this case ensured that private schools in India are held to the same standards as public institutions when it comes to protecting students' rights


    Pradeep Kumar Biswas v. Indian Institute of Chemical Biology (2002)


    In Pradeep Kumar Biswas v. Indian Institute of Chemical Biology (2002), the Supreme Court examined whether the Council of Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) qualifies as a "State" under Article 12 of the Indian Constitution. The case arose when employees challenged their termination by CSIR. Previously, in Sabhajit Tewary's case, CSIR was deemed not a "State," but this ruling was contested.

    The Supreme Court found that CSIR is significantly controlled and funded by the government, thus classifying it as a "State." This landmark decision overruled the earlier judgment and clarified that entities with substantial government involvement can be considered "State" under Article 12, ensuring employees' rights to seek legal remedies against such bodies.

    Keshavan Madhava Menon v. State of Bombay (1951)


    In Keshavan Madhava Menon v. State of Bombay (1951), the Supreme Court addressed whether laws that violate fundamental rights are considered void from the beginning (ab initio) or only ineffective going forward. The case involved a challenge to the Indian Press (Emergency Powers) Act.

    The Court ruled that laws inconsistent with fundamental rights do not become void from the start but are ineffective prospectively. This means that if someone committed an offense under such a law before it was declared void, they could still be prosecuted. Essentially, the ruling clarified that while laws can be declared void due to inconsistency with fundamental rights, they remain applicable to past actions until that declaration is made.


    Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)


    In Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973), the Supreme Court decided that Parliament can change the Constitution but cannot alter its "basic structure," which includes key features like democracy and fundamental rights. The case arose when Kesavananda Bharati challenged a law that took over land from his monastery. This ruling established the basic structure doctrine, protecting essential parts of the Constitution from being changed by Parliament.

    2 Important Aspect
    • Doctrine of Eclipse

    The Doctrine of Eclipse is a legal principle in India that deals with laws that conflict with fundamental rights. Here’s a simple breakdown:

    What it means :

    If a law is made that goes against the fundamental rights guaranteed by the Constitution, that law doesn’t become completely void or dead. Instead, it becomes "eclipsed," like how the sun is hidden during a solar eclipse.

    Temporary Effect :

    While the law is eclipsed, it cannot be enforced. However, it can come back into effect if the conflict with fundamental rights is resolved.

    Pre constitutional Law

    This doctrine mainly applies to laws that were made before the Constitution came into effect in 1950. These laws can be revived if they are amended to align with fundamental rights.

    Judicial Role

    The courts play an important role in deciding whether a law is eclipsed and whether it can be revived.

    • Doctrine of Severability


    The Doctrine of Severability is a legal principle that allows courts to separate unconstitutional provisions of a law from its valid parts. Here’s a concise overview:

    Separation of Provisions

    If a specific provision of a statute is found to violate the Constitution, only that provision is declared void, while the rest of the statute remains effective.

    Application

    This doctrine applies when valid and invalid parts can be clearly distinguished. If they are interlinked and cannot be separated, the entire statute may be struck down.

    Constitutional Basis

    Article 13 of the Indian Constitution supports this doctrine by stating that laws inconsistent with fundamental rights are void only to the extent of their inconsistency.

    Judicial Interpretation

    Courts determine whether the invalid portion can be severed without altering the law's overall intent or functionality

    Ram Krishna Dalmia v. Justice Tendolkar (1958)


    In the case of Ram Krishna Dalmia v. Justice Tendolkar (1958), the Supreme Court focused on the principle of equality under Article 14 of the Constitution. The Court introduced the "classification test," which allows the government to treat different groups differently, as long as this classification is based on clear differences and serves a valid purpose.

    The Court struck down a law because it gave the government too much power to classify offenses without clear guidelines, leading to arbitrary treatment. This case laid the groundwork for understanding how equality and reasonable classification work in Indian law.


    Indira Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)


    In Indira Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975), the Supreme Court examined the validity of the 39th Amendment, which aimed to protect Indira Gandhi's election from being challenged in court. Raj Narain contested her election, claiming electoral misconduct.

    The Court ruled that the amendment violated Article 14, which guarantees equality before the law. It stated that no one, including the Prime Minister, should be above the law. The ruling emphasized that laws must apply equally to everyone and cannot provide special privileges to any individual. This case reinforced the principle of accountability in governance and upheld the right to equality.

      In DP Joshi v. State of Madhya Bharat (1955), the Supreme Court dealt with a challenge to a government order reserving seats in medical colleges for backward classes. Joshi argued that this reservation violated Article 15(1), which prohibits discrimination based on religion, race, caste, or place of birth.

      The Court upheld the reservation, stating that Article 15(1) does not prevent the state from making special provisions for the advancement of socially and educationally backward classes. The ruling emphasized that such provisions are valid if they aim to improve educational opportunities for these groups, thereby promoting equality in society.


      State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan (1951)


      In State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan (1951), the Supreme Court addressed a case where Champakam Dorairajan, a Brahmin woman, was denied admission to a medical college due to a government policy that reserved seats based on caste. She argued that this policy violated her right to equality under Articles 15(1) and 29(2) of the Constitution.

      The Court ruled that the reservation policy was unconstitutional because it discriminated against her based on caste.

      This landmark decision emphasized that fundamental rights, like equality, take precedence over state policies aimed at social welfare. The ruling led to significant changes in how reservations were implemented in India.

      Balaji v. State of Mysore (1963)

      In Balaji v. State of Mysore (1963), the Supreme Court examined the issue of reservations in public employment for backward classes. The case arose when the government reserved a certain percentage of jobs for these classes.

      The Court upheld the idea of reservations but set a limit, stating that the total reservation should not exceed 50%. This ruling aimed to balance the need for affirmative action with the principle of equality, ensuring that reservations do not unfairly disadvantage other groups.

      The decision clarified that while special provisions for backward classes are allowed, they must be reasonable and not excessive.


      Indira Sahani v. Union of India (1992)

      In Indira Sahani v. Union of India (1992), also known as the Mandal Commission case, the Supreme Court addressed the issue of reservations for socially and educationally backward classes (SEBCs) in government jobs.

      The Court upheld the idea of reservations to promote social justice but set important guidelines. It ruled that reservations should not exceed 50% of total positions to maintain equality and prevent reverse discrimination.

      The Court also stated that only the "creamy layer" (the more advanced members of backward classes) should be excluded from these benefits to ensure that truly disadvantaged individuals receive support. This landmark ruling clarified how reservations should be implemented in India.

      People's Union for Democratic Rights v. Union of India (1982)

      In People's Union for Democratic Rights v. Union of India (1982), the Supreme Court addressed the issue of untouchability and discrimination against marginalized communities. The case involved workers from the Scheduled Castes who were denied basic rights and faced discrimination.

      The Court ruled that the state has a duty to protect individuals from violations of their rights, even when these violations are committed by private individuals. It emphasized that untouchability is not just a historical issue but continues to affect people today. This ruling reinforced the government's responsibility to ensure equality and prevent discrimination based on caste, aligning with Article 17 of the Constitution, which abolishes untouchability.


      State of Karnataka v. Appa Balu Ingale (2006)

      In State of Karnataka v. Appa Balu Ingale (2006), the Supreme Court addressed the ongoing practice of untouchability. The case involved individuals from a marginalized community who were prevented from using water from a newly dug borewell because of their caste status.

      The Court ruled that such discrimination is a form of modern-day slavery and an extension of the caste system, which is prohibited under Article 17 of the Constitution. It emphasized that untouchability practices must be eradicated and that the state has a responsibility to protect individuals from caste-based discrimination. This decision reinforced the legal stance against untouchability and highlighted the need for social change.

      Balaji Raghavan v. Union of India (1995)

      In the case of Balaji Raghavan v. Union of India (1995), the Supreme Court addressed whether national awards like the Bharat Ratna and Padma Bhushan are considered titles under Article 18 of the Indian Constitution, which prohibits the government from conferring titles.

      Challenge to Awards: The petitioners argued that these awards should be banned because they fall under the definition of titles prohibited by Article 18.

      supreme court's decision

      The Court ruled that national awards do not violate Article 18. It stated that recognizing individuals for their exceptional service does not create social hierarchies, which Article 18 aims to prevent.

      Encouraging Excellence

      The Court emphasized that the principle of equality does not mean denying recognition to those who have contributed significantly to society.

      Recommendation

      It suggested forming a commission to ensure that only deserving individuals receive these awards, preventing misuse.

      This case clarified that while Article 18 aims to abolish titles, it does not restrict acknowledging outstanding contributions to the nation.


      Indira Jaising v. Supreme Court of India (2017)

      The case of Indira Jaising v. Supreme Court of India (2017) is significant because it deals with the designation of Senior Advocates in the legal profession and whether this practice goes against the Constitution.

      What was the Issue ?

      In India, some lawyers are given the title of Senior Advocate based on their experience and contributions to the legal field. Jaising challenged this practice, arguing that it could create inequalities among lawyers and violate Article 18 of the Constitution, which prohibits titles that create social hierarchies.

      Main ArgumentsTitle vs. Recognition:

      Jaising argued that calling someone a Senior Advocate is like giving them a title, which could lead to a sense of superiority over other lawyers. She believed this could undermine the principle of equality.

      Lack of Transparency:

      She pointed out that the process for becoming a Senior Advocate was not clear or fair. This could lead to favoritism and unfair practices, where only certain lawyers get recognized while others do not.

      Impact on Public Interest Litigation (PIL):

      Jaising emphasized that if Senior Advocates are seen as more important, it might discourage others from participating in important legal cases that affect the public.

      Supreme Court Decision Recognition of Merit

      The Supreme Court ruled that the designation of Senior Advocates is not a title in the traditional sense but rather a recognition of a lawyer's skill, experience, and contributions to law.

      Not against Article 18

      The Court stated that this designation does not violate Article 18 because it does not create a social hierarchy or inequality among lawyers.

      Importance of Senior Advocates

      The Court acknowledged that Senior Advocates play a crucial role in the legal system, especially in complex cases, and their expertise helps ensure justice.

      Need for Fair Processes

      While upholding the designation, the Court also recognized the need for transparency in how Senior Advocates are appointed. It encouraged better guidelines to ensure fairness in the selection process.

      Public Interest Litigation (PIL):

      The Court emphasized that PILs are essential for promoting social justice and that having experienced lawyers involved can help address important issues effectively.

      Conclusion

      The case clarified that recognizing skilled lawyers as Senior Advocates is acceptable under Indian law as long as it focuses on their qualifications and contributions rather than creating divisions among them.

      State of Bombay v. Kathi Kalu Oghad

      The case State of Bombay v. Kathi Kalu Oghad (1961) is significant in Indian law, particularly concerning the rights of accused individuals under Article 20(3) of the Constitution, which protects against self-incrimination

      Background

      Kathi Kalu Oghad and others were accused of murder. During the investigation, the police obtained handwriting samples from Oghad while he was in custody. The prosecution wanted to use these samples as evidence to prove his involvement in the crime

      Legal Issue

      Oghad argued that taking his handwriting samples violated Article 20(3), which states that no person accused of a crime can be compelled to be a witness against themselves. The key question was whether providing handwriting samples constituted self-incrimination

      High court

      On appeal, the High Court ruled that the samples were inadmissible because they were taken while Oghad was in police custody, implying an element of compulsion. The High Court acquitted him due to insufficient evidence without the handwriting samples.

      supreme court Ruling

      The State of Bombay appealed to the Supreme Court, which had to decide on several crucial points

      self incrimination

      The Supreme Court ruled that providing physical evidence like fingerprints or handwriting samples does not fall under "being a witness against oneself." This means that such evidence can be obtained even if the person is in custody.

      Distinction

      The Court distinguished between verbal testimony (which could incriminate) and physical evidence (which does not involve self-testimony). Thus, taking fingerprints or handwriting samples does not violate Article 20(3).

      outcome

      The Supreme Court upheld the trial court's decision regarding the admissibility of physical specimens but noted that the High Court's acquittal was based on insufficient evidence without those specimens

      Conculsion

      The ruling in this case clarified that while individuals have rights against self-incrimination, those rights do not extend to physical evidence collection like fingerprints or handwriting samples. This case is pivotal for understanding how constitutional protections apply in criminal proceedings and has implications for law enforcement practices in India.

        Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India

        The Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India case (1983) is a landmark Supreme Court decision in India that addressed the issue of bonded laborers.

        Background

        Bandhua Mukti Morcha, a non-governmental organization (NGO), wrote a letter to the Supreme Court about bonded laborers working in terrible conditions at stone quarries in Faridabad, Haryana. Many of these laborers were trapped in a system where they had to work to pay off debts, often in unsafe and unhealthy environments.

        Supreme court's decision

        The Supreme Court treated the letter as a formal petition under Article 32 of the Constitution, which allows individuals to seek justice for violations of their fundamental rights. The court appointed a commission to investigate the conditions at the quarries.

        The investigation confirmed that the laborers were indeed working under inhumane conditions, with no access to clean water or proper shelter. The court ruled that the state government had to take immediate action to protect these workers' rights.

        Key orders

        • Establish Vigilance Committees: The Haryana government was ordered to set up committees to monitor and ensure compliance with labor laws.
        • Identify Bonded Laborers: District magistrates were tasked with identifying bonded laborers.

        • Rehabilitation: The government had to provide housing and support for the affected workers.
        • Implement Minimum Wages: Both state and central governments were directed to enforce minimum wage laws for these workers.

        Significance

        This case was crucial because it expanded the concept of public interest litigation (PIL) in India, allowing NGOs and individuals to represent the rights of marginalized groups. It highlighted the importance of protecting human rights and ensuring justice for vulnerable populations, making it easier for such cases to be heard in court.

        In the Prithi Pal Singh Bedi vs Union of India (1982) case, the Supreme Court upheld the power of Parliament to restrict fundamental rights for armed forces personnel under Article 33 of the Indian Constitution.         Babita Puniya case

        The Babita Puniya case is a landmark legal decision in India that significantly advanced women's rights in the military.

        Background

        In February 2003, Babita Puniya, a practicing lawyer, filed a petition in the Delhi High Court. She sought to grant permanent commissions (PC) to women officers in the Indian Army, arguing that they should have the same opportunities as their male counterparts.

        Key Developments

        • 2003-2010: Several women officers joined Babita's cause, filing petitions for equal rights. The Delhi High Court ruled in favor of granting permanent commissions to women officers, but the government did not implement this decision.
        • 2011: The case reached the Supreme Court after the government challenged the High Court’s ruling. The Supreme Court upheld the earlier decision, emphasizing that denying women permanent commissions was discriminatory.
        • 2019: The government announced guidelines for granting permanent commissions but limited them to new recruits, excluding those already serving.

        Supreme court Judgment


        On February 17, 2020, the Supreme Court delivered its judgment:

        The court ruled that women officers must be granted permanent commissions and should be eligible for command positions.

        Justice D.Y. Chandrachud stated that restrictions based on gender stereotypes were unconstitutional and violated women's fundamental rights under Article 14 (right to equality).

        The court acknowledged that women had been serving effectively in the army and deserved equal opportunities without discrimination.

        Impact

        This ruling was a significant step towards gender equality in the Indian military:

        • It allowed women to hold higher ranks and receive benefits like promotions and pensions.
        • The judgment challenged outdated perceptions of women's roles in the military and recognized their capabilities.

        Conclusion

        The Babita Puniya case marked a pivotal moment for women's rights in India, ensuring that female officers could serve on equal footing with male officers in all aspects of military service. It set a precedent for further advancements in gender equality within the armed forces and beyond.
